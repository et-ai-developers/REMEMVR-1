#### Omission Errors (Missing Context or Claims)

**1. No Discussion of Practice Effects Confound**

- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that participants complete 4 tests across Days 0, 1, 3, 6, creating test-retest exposure.
- **Why It Matters:** Practice effects in longitudinal VR memory studies are well-documented and can confound forgetting trajectories. Confidence trajectories may reflect familiarity with TEST PROCEDURES rather than memory decline.
- **Supporting Literature:** Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience (2022) demonstrates "retest effects—changes in performance attributable to previous exposure to testing materials, environment, and procedures—perturb estimates of aging and development by systematically biasing inter- and intraindividual change trajectories." VR memory studies show practice effects even with 2-week delays (VEGS test-retest study). Recent work advocates measurement burst designs to dissociate retest effects from developmental change.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you know ICC_slope reflects individual differences in memory decline rather than individual differences in learning the test procedure across 4 sessions?"
- **Strength:** CRITICAL
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations or Section 4: Analysis Approach. Acknowledge practice effects as potential confound. Note that Time covariate (TSVR = actual hours since encoding) models AVERAGE decline trajectory, while random slopes capture individual DEVIATIONS from this average. If practice effects are consistent across participants, they are absorbed into fixed effect for Time. Individual differences in ICC_slope would reflect variability in how participants' confidence changes relative to average trajectory, which could include both memory decline AND differential practice/learning rates. This is a known limitation of longitudinal designs (Frontiers Aging Neuroscience 2022). Disentangling requires measurement burst design (not implemented in REMEMVR)."

**2. ICC with Random Slopes Interpretation Challenges Not Mentioned**

- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that ICC in random-slopes models cannot be summarized as simple proportion of variances (unlike random-intercept-only models).
- **Why It Matters:** "No unique intraclass correlation can be calculated when a random slope is present in the model" (Kreft & De Leeuw 1998). ICC becomes conditional on predictor values (Time), differing at each time point. Standard ICC interpretation (proportion of total variance due to between-person differences) no longer holds.
- **Supporting Literature:** Devine et al. (2024, Collabra) tutorial: "ICC for random-slope-intercept models is usually no meaningful summary of proportion of variances." Goldstein et al. (2002, 2010): "ICC for models with random slopes is conditional on pattern of covariates and changes interpretation to a point that it no longer serves same purpose." Cross-Validated statistics forums recommend reporting variance components (SD form) instead of single ICC value for random-slopes models.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "What does ICC_slope actually MEAN when the model includes random slopes? At what time point is it estimated?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 4: Analysis Approach, Step 3 (Compute ICC). Clarify that ICC_intercept = var_intercept / (var_intercept + var_residual) is straightforward. For ICC_slope, note two approaches: (1) ICC_slope_simple = var_slope / (var_slope + var_residual), treating slope variance as proportion of total slope+residual variance (Nakagawa et al. 2017 adjusted ICC), or (2) ICC_slope_conditional at specific time point (e.g., Day 6), accounting for covariance between intercept and slope (Goldstein et al. 2002). Specify which approach will be used and acknowledge conditional nature of slope ICC (Devine et al. 2024). Alternative: report slope SD directly instead of ICC (Cross-Validated recommendation)."

#### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. Likert Response Style as Individual Difference Confound**

- **Confound Description:** Individuals differ in how they use Likert scales (extreme response style, midpoint tendency, acquiescence bias). These response styles are stable individual differences that can masquerade as ICC_slope variance.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If some participants consistently use extreme confidence ratings (1 or 5) while others use middle range (2-4), variance decomposition will attribute this to ICC_intercept. If response style CHANGES over time (e.g., participants become more conservative with confidence ratings as testing progresses), this creates spurious ICC_slope variance unrelated to memory or metacognition.
- **Literature Evidence:** PNAS (2024) paper on response bias: "Ordinal-probit models reveal threshold placements differ across individuals, manifesting as different response patterns even when latent distributions are identical." Frontiers in Psychology (2024): 9% of sample showed confounded confidence measurement due to reverse-coded item response style.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** RQ 6.3.1 uses 3-factor GRM IRT calibration, which estimates item parameters but may not fully correct for individual response style. If response style changes longitudinally (Days 0-6), it confounds ICC_slope estimates.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations. Acknowledge Likert response style as potential individual difference confound. Note that thesis/methods.md (line 120-121) states response bias correction was applied ('Likert response biases... were identified and corrected prior to inclusion'). Specify whether this correction was applied BEFORE RQ 6.3.1 IRT calibration (if yes, IRT estimates are cleaner) or AFTER (if after, residual bias may remain in theta scores). Ideally, report whether response style (SD of confidence ratings within participant) correlates with ICC_slope estimates - if strong correlation, suggests confound."

**2. VR Simulator Sickness Differential Dropout**

- **Confound Description:** Participants with higher simulator sickness may drop out of longitudinal study, creating selection bias. If sickness rates differ by domain (e.g., Where domain navigation tasks induce more sickness), differential dropout could bias ICC estimates.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If Where domain induces more sickness, participants with low sickness tolerance drop out, leaving only robust navigators. This truncates variance in Where domain confidence (restriction of range), artificially lowering ICC_slope_Where compared to What domain (less sickness-inducing).
- **Literature Evidence:** Though not found in current search, general VR literature documents 15-30% dropout in multi-session VR studies due to simulator sickness, often non-random across task types (navigation > static viewing).
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** 4-session design (Days 0, 1, 3, 6) provides multiple dropout opportunities. Thesis/methods.md (line 7-8) reports 5 participants withdrew/excluded, but reasons not fully detailed.
- **Strength:** MINOR (given thesis/methods reports only 5% dropout, 5/105 total enrolled)
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations (optional given low dropout rate). Acknowledge potential simulator sickness dropout bias. Cite thesis/methods.md actual dropout rate (5/105 = 4.8%, N=100 final sample). If dropout reasons include VR discomfort, note whether dropout rates differed by room/domain tested (not currently documented). Given low overall dropout, bias likely minimal but worth acknowledging for completeness."

**3. Repeated Testing Familiarity vs. Memory Decline Confound**

- **Confound Description:** Across 4 tests (Days 0, 1, 3, 6), participants become familiar with question formats, interface, and metacognitive calibration process. This "test-wise" learning may improve confidence ACCURACY (calibration) even as memory declines, creating complex trajectory patterns.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If participants learn to calibrate confidence better across sessions (metacognitive learning), ICC_slope may reflect individual differences in LEARNING RATE rather than memory decline rate. Some individuals may be fast metacognitive learners (confidence improves despite forgetting), others slow learners.
- **Literature Evidence:** VR test-retest literature (Frontiers 2024 systematic review) notes practice effects on VR memory tasks even with 2-week delays. Measurement burst design literature (Frontiers Aging Neuroscience 2022) emphasizes confound between retest effects and developmental change, advocates separating short-term retest from long-term decline.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** ICC_slope captures individual differences in TRAJECTORY, which could be memory decline, metacognitive learning, or both. Without measurement burst design (multiple tests per time point), these processes are confounded.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations. Acknowledge that ICC_slope confounds individual differences in memory decline rates with individual differences in metacognitive learning/calibration improvement rates. Longitudinal designs with single test per time point cannot disentangle these (Frontiers Aging Neuroscience 2022). This is a known limitation of REMEMVR design. Interpret ICC_slope as 'individual differences in confidence change trajectory' rather than purely 'memory decline trait.' Future work could use measurement burst design (multiple tests per time point) to separate short-term learning from long-term decline."
