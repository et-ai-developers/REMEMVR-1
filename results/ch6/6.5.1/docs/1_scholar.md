#### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**1. Oversimplified Schema-Accuracy NULL Hypothesis**

- **Location:** 1_concept.md - Section 3: Hypothesis - "NULL expected: Schema × Time interaction will be non-significant (p > 0.05), paralleling Ch5 5.4.1 accuracy findings"
- **Claim Made:** Schema congruence has no effect on accuracy trajectories (simple NULL hypothesis)
- **Scholarly Criticism:** Recent research shows schema effects on accuracy are RECOLLECTION-DEPENDENT, not universally NULL. Ramey et al. (2022, *Cognition*) found that schema congruency had NO effect for recollected scenes (supporting NULL), but DID affect accuracy for familiarity-based memory and scenes with no underlying memory. The NULL pattern emerges only when recollection is strong.
- **Counterevidence:** Ramey et al. (2022): "Schema congruency had no significant effect on spatial accuracy for scenes that were recollected... analysis using Bayes factors provided substantial evidence for the null hypothesis. However, when participants had no underlying memory for the scenes, there were strong schema effects on the final memory responses in that spatial accuracy for the target location was substantially better for congruent than incongruent scenes." This U-shaped pattern (schema effects for weak memory, NULL for strong recollection) contradicts the simple NULL hypothesis.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** "Qualify the NULL hypothesis: Schema × Time interaction may be NULL at the aggregate level (as observed in Ch5 5.4.1), but this could mask heterogeneity across memory quality levels. If REMEMVR participants have high recollection rates (due to immersive encoding), the aggregate NULL pattern would be consistent with Ramey et al. (2022). Add post-hoc analysis plan: stratify by confidence level (high confidence = likely recollection, low confidence = likely familiarity/guessing) to test whether schema effects emerge for low-confidence trials. This would demonstrate sophisticated understanding of dual-process mechanisms."

**2. Unitized Encoding Mechanism Underspecified**

- **Location:** 1_concept.md - Section 3: Hypothesis - "Unitized encoding in immersive VR eliminates schema-based differences"
- **Claim Made:** Immersive VR creates "unitized encoding" that eliminates schema effects
- **Scholarly Criticism:** The concept of "unitized encoding" in VR is not well-defined in memory literature. While VR provides immersive, ecologically valid encoding contexts, the specific mechanism by which VR immersion affects schema integration (hippocampal binding vs neocortical schema activation) is not specified. The claim conflates ecological validity (real-world-like encoding) with a specific memory mechanism (unitization).
- **Counterevidence:** VR memory research (Frontiers 2024 systematic review) notes that "differences may exist in the manner in which VR environments are encoded by brain areas such as the hippocampus, when compared with the use of real-world environments." The assumption that VR creates hippocampus-dependent episodic binding that overrides schema-based neocortical processing is plausible but unverified.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** "Revise hypothesis language: Replace 'unitized encoding in immersive VR' with 'hippocampus-dependent episodic binding during immersive VR encoding may reduce reliance on schema-based neocortical processing.' Acknowledge that this mechanism is hypothesized but not empirically validated for REMEMVR. #### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Encoding Quality Differences (Not Schema-Based Fluency)**

- **Alternative Theory:** Differences attributed to "schema-based fluency" at retrieval might actually reflect ENCODING QUALITY differences. Schema-congruent items may be encoded more richly (deeper processing, more elaborative rehearsal) during the 10-minute room exploration, creating stronger initial memory traces that are ACCURATELY reflected in higher confidence.
- **How It Applies:** If schema-congruent placements (e.g., umbrella near door) elicit more semantic elaboration during encoding ("that makes sense, umbrellas belong near doors"), this could create genuinely stronger memories (not just fluency-based illusion). Higher confidence for congruent items would then reflect ACCURATE metacognitive monitoring of stronger memory, not fluency-based bias.
- **Key Citation:** Ramey et al. (2022) discussion: "Prior knowledge aids memory for both congruent and incongruent events, but in different ways." Congruent items benefit from semantic integration during encoding (deeper processing), while incongruent items benefit from distinctiveness (shallow perceptual salience). Confidence for congruent items may reflect semantic encoding depth, not retrieval fluency.
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Reviewers will question whether proposed "fluency heuristic" is actually encoding quality. The NULL accuracy effect (Ch5 5.4.1) argues against encoding quality differences, but this needs explicit discussion.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background - 'An alternative explanation is that schema-congruent items receive deeper semantic encoding (elaborative rehearsal), creating genuinely stronger memories that are accurately reflected in higher confidence. However, Ch5 5.4.1 found NULL schema-congruence effects on ACCURACY, suggesting that encoding quality does not differ systematically by congruence level. If confidence shows schema effects where accuracy does not, this dissociation supports the fluency heuristic (retrieval ease misattributed to memory strength) rather than encoding quality differences.'"

**2. Response Bias / Scale Use Differences (Not Metacognitive Monitoring)**

- **Alternative Theory:** Schema-congruent items may elicit higher confidence ratings due to RESPONSE BIAS (preference for extreme scale points when answer "feels right") rather than actual metacognitive monitoring of memory strength.
- **How It Applies:** Research on Likert scales (Frontiers 2021 GRM study) notes that "when you give respondents a rating scale, they tend to opt for the middle choice or agree with most statements" (response bias). If schema-congruent items feel more "natural" or "obvious," participants may use extreme confidence ratings (5-star "Absolutely Certain") more liberally, even if actual memory strength is equivalent.
- **Key Citation:** Frontiers (2021) on GRM: "A Likert scale is an ordinal (i.e., ordered categorical) scale by definition, where the difference between any two adjacent response options is not necessarily the same difference as between another pair of adjacent options." IRT models (GRM) account for this by estimating item-specific difficulty thresholds (b1, b2, b3, b4 for 5-category scale), but individual-level response bias (tendency to use extreme vs mid-range ratings) is a separate issue.
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Response bias would be a methodological confound (not theoretically interesting), unlike fluency heuristic (theoretically interesting metacognitive process). IRT theta scoring partly controls for response bias by modeling item thresholds, but individual differences in scale use remain.
- **Strength:** MINOR (IRT calibration addresses this partially)
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations - 'Confidence ratings may be affected by response bias (preference for extreme vs mid-range scale points) independent of actual metacognitive monitoring. #### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. GRM Sample Size Requirements for Stable Calibration**

- **Confound Description:** GRM calibration for 5-category Likert scales requires sample size >= 300 for stable item parameter estimates (Frontiers 2021). REMEMVR has N=100 participants × 4 tests = 400 observations (above threshold), but item purification (Step 2, Decision D039) may reduce effective sample if 30-70% of items are purified.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If many items are purified (e.g., 50% retention rate), effective sample per factor may drop below N=300 (e.g., if 10 items purified to 5 items, and each item has 400 responses, total observations = 2000, but per-item calibration quality depends on item-level sample size). Unstable item parameters could lead to noisy theta estimates, increasing SE and reducing power to detect Schema × Time interaction.
- **Literature Evidence:** Frontiers (2021): "For item parameter estimations, results suggest a sample size of at least 300 and/or an instrument length of at least five items for both models... - **Why Relevant to This RQ:** RQ 6.5.1 uses 3-factor GRM (Common/Congruent/Incongruent), meaning item-level sample size is split across factors. **2. Confidence Calibration Across Test Sessions (Metacognitive Learning)**

- **Literature Evidence:** Frontiers (2024) metacognition review: "Survey measures of metacognitive monitoring are often false... those who reported being better at metacognitive monitoring tended to be worse according to the online metacognitive ratings." - **Why Relevant to This RQ:** The hypothesis predicts NULL Schema × Time interaction (confidence declines at same rate for all congruence levels). - **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations - 'Repeated testing (4 sessions across 6 days) may enable metacognitive calibration learning, where participants adjust confidence ratings based on implicit feedback from prior tests.
