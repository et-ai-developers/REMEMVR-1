### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**1. Oversimplified Metacognitive Monitoring Theory**

- **Location:** Section 2: Theoretical Background, paragraph 2
- **Claim Made:** "Confidence judgments reflect underlying memory strength"
- **Scholarly Criticism:** This characterization oversimplifies metacognitive monitoring theory. Recent research shows confidence judgments reflect MULTIPLE cues, not just memory trace strength. Fleming & Daw (2024) show confidence is "informed by the observer's models of the world and their cognitive system, which may be more or less accurate—thus explaining why metacognitive judgments are inferential and sometimes diverge from task performance."
- **Counterevidence:** The two-stage processing model (BMC Psychology, 2024) demonstrates confidence involves both retrieval processes AND monitoring processes, with distinct neural signatures (N400 for retrieval, slow-wave for monitoring). Cue familiarity research shows confidence can be driven by familiarity independent of actual memory strength.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** Revise to "Confidence judgments reflect INFERENCES about underlying memory strength based on multiple cues (retrieval fluency, cue familiarity, trace accessibility). While not perfect indicators of memory accuracy, confidence should still track consolidation-driven changes in memory strength if metacognitive monitoring is sensitive to trace stability."

**2. Missing Acknowledgment of Cue Familiarity Effects**

- **Location:** Section 2: Theoretical Background, paragraph 3 ("Theoretical Predictions")
- **Claim Made:** Confidence decline should show two-phase pattern paralleling accuracy
- **Scholarly Criticism:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that confidence judgments may be driven by cue familiarity rather than trace strength changes. Research shows "cue familiarity but not target retrievability enhances feeling-of-knowing judgments" (Reder & Ritter, 1992, still relevant framework). If cue familiarity decays differently than memory traces, confidence trajectories may NOT parallel accuracy trajectories even with valid consolidation effects.
- **Counterevidence:** The two-stage confidence processing model (2024) shows prospective metacognitive monitoring relies on encoding fluency and familiarity cues, while retrospective monitoring uses retrieval fluency and trace strength. If early retention intervals favor cue-familiarity-based confidence while late intervals favor trace-strength-based confidence, the transition might create apparent two-phase pattern for DIFFERENT reasons than consolidation.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** "Add to Section 2: Acknowledge cue familiarity as alternative mechanism. State: 'If confidence judgments at early intervals (Day 0-1) rely more on cue familiarity while late intervals (Day 1-6) rely more on trace strength monitoring, observed two-phase pattern could reflect shift in metacognitive cue usage rather than consolidation. However, if BOTH accuracy and confidence show parallel two-phase patterns, this suggests consolidation affects both trace accessibility AND metacognitive cues proportionally.'"

### Omission Errors (Missing Context or Claims)

**1. No Discussion of Practice Effects on Metacognitive Calibration**

- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that repeated testing (4 sessions) may affect metacognitive calibration independently of memory decay
- **Why It Matters:** VR research shows "participants who undergo VR interventions exhibited more confidence when conducting tasks in real world" (BMC Psychiatry, 2024 systematic review). If practice with VR testing INCREASES confidence independent of memory strength, this could mask or distort confidence decline trajectories.
- **Supporting Literature:** Neural plasticity research shows "frequent training reinforces neural plasticity, strengthens synaptic connections" in VR contexts. Metacognitive confidence can improve through test familiarity even as memory decays, creating dissociation between confidence and accuracy trajectories.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you distinguish genuine confidence decline from practice-driven confidence increases that partially compensate for memory decay?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 4: Analysis Approach - Acknowledge practice effects: 'Repeated testing may increase metacognitive calibration (practice with confidence rating scale) while memory decays, potentially attenuating confidence decline relative to accuracy decline. IRT theta scores partially control for this by separating person ability from item difficulty, but test session as covariate in LMM may further isolate memory-driven confidence changes from practice-driven calibration improvements.'"

**2. Missing Discussion of Encoding Quality Confound**

- **Missing Content:** No acknowledgment that confidence judgments may reflect encoding quality differences rather than consolidation-driven decay
- **Why It Matters:** Research shows confidence judgments rely on "encoding fluency and familiarity" cues (two-stage confidence model, 2024). If items encoded with high initial confidence (strong encoding) show different decay trajectories than items encoded with low confidence (weak encoding), observed confidence trajectories may reflect heterogeneous initial states rather than consolidation processes.
- **Supporting Literature:** Fleming & Daw (2024): "Confidence is informed by observer's models of cognitive system, which may be more or less accurate." Initial encoding quality affects both memory strength AND metacognitive confidence, potentially creating spurious two-phase patterns if high-confidence items are consolidated preferentially.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "Do confidence trajectories reflect decay of uniformly encoded items, or do they reflect differential forgetting of heterogeneously encoded items sorted by initial confidence?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background - Acknowledge encoding quality: 'Confidence judgments at Day 0 reflect both memory strength and encoding quality. If high-confidence items are preferentially consolidated during sleep (adaptive consolidation), observed two-phase pattern could reflect differential retention of high- vs. low-confidence items rather than uniform decay with consolidation breakpoint. However, using ALL items (omnibus factor) averages across encoding quality, and Chapter 5 comparison allows testing whether confidence trajectories parallel accuracy trajectories despite encoding heterogeneity.'"

**3. No Acknowledgment of Likert Response Bias in Ordinal Confidence Scales**

- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not discuss known biases in Likert-scale confidence ratings (anchoring, social desirability, acquiescence)
- **Why It Matters:** Recent PNAS paper (2024) shows "Likert-type scales are plagued by confounding effects of response bias." Anchoring effects cause "extremes of Likert-type response to get less use than central choices, creating intervals near extremes that are further apart than those near center" (literature review, 2024). This non-linearity could distort IRT theta estimates if response bias changes across test sessions.
- **Supporting Literature:** Response bias research shows social desirability (SDR) and acquiescent response (ACQ) affect Likert scales. If participants become more conservative with confidence ratings across repeated testing (anchoring shifts), apparent confidence decline may partially reflect response style changes rather than memory decay.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "Did you check for response bias patterns (e.g., participants who only use extreme categories, or who shift anchoring across sessions)? How does this affect IRT theta estimates?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 4: Analysis Approach (or Section 7: Limitations) - Acknowledge Likert bias: 'Methods.md reports Likert response biases were identified and corrected prior to IRT analysis (participants using only extreme or narrow confidence bands). IRT graded response model partially controls for response style by separating item thresholds (category boundaries) from person ability (theta), but remaining response bias (e.g., anchoring shifts across sessions) could affect trajectory estimates. Sensitivity analyses may test whether excluding participants with extreme response styles alters two-phase pattern conclusions.'"

### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Cue-Based Metacognitive Models vs. Trace-Strength Models**

- **Alternative Theory:** Cue-based models propose confidence reflects accessibility of metacognitive cues (familiarity, fluency) rather than direct access to memory trace strength
- **How It Applies:** If confidence judgments rely on cue familiarity (which may decay independently of memory traces), confidence trajectories may NOT parallel accuracy trajectories even with valid consolidation effects. Two-phase pattern in confidence could reflect: (1) rapid cue familiarity decay Day 0-1 (cues lose accessibility), (2) slow trace-strength monitoring Day 1-6 (shift to retrieval-based confidence). This is a DIFFERENT mechanism than consolidation affecting both accuracy and confidence proportionally.
- **Key Citation:** Research on "cue familiarity but not target retrievability enhances feeling-of-knowing" (classic framework still influential). Recent 2024 work on two-stage confidence processing shows "prospective metacognitive monitoring relies on encoding fluency and familiarity cues, while retrospective monitoring uses retrieval fluency and trace strength."
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Reviewers familiar with cue-based metacognition may argue observed two-phase pattern reflects cue-type shift (familiarity->strength) rather than consolidation dynamics. Need to distinguish these mechanisms.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background - Discuss cue-based alternatives: 'Alternative cue-based models propose confidence reflects cue familiarity accessibility rather than direct trace strength monitoring. Under this framework, two-phase confidence pattern could reflect shift from familiarity-based confidence (Days 0-1) to retrieval-strength-based confidence (Days 1-6) rather than consolidation-driven decay. However, if accuracy ALSO shows two-phase pattern (Ch5 5.1.2), and confidence trajectories PARALLEL accuracy trajectories, this suggests consolidation affects both memory traces and metacognitive cues proportionally, supporting trace-strength interpretation over cue-shift interpretation.'"

### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. Ordinal Data Non-Linearity in IRT Theta Estimates**

- **Confound Description:** IRT graded response model assumes ordinal categories reflect latent continuous ability, but Likert response biases (anchoring, extreme avoidance) create non-linear mappings between observed ratings and theta scores
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If participants' use of confidence categories changes across sessions (e.g., becoming more conservative, avoiding extremes after practice), IRT theta estimates may reflect response style changes as much as memory strength changes. This could create spurious trajectory patterns if response bias shifts systematically with retention interval.
- **Literature Evidence:** Recent work shows "intervals near extremes may be further apart than those near center" due to anchoring effects. IRT models with location-scale extensions can capture within-subject variance in category usage, but standard GRM (used in RQ 6.1.1) does not model response style directly.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** If response style shifts at Day 1 (after first sleep, participants recalibrate confidence scale usage), this could create apparent breakpoint in confidence trajectories independent of consolidation.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations - Acknowledge IRT assumptions: 'IRT graded response model assumes ordinal confidence categories map linearly to latent theta ability. However, response style changes (anchoring shifts, extreme avoidance) across sessions could affect theta estimates if participants recalibrate confidence scale usage after initial testing. Methods.md reports response bias correction was applied, and RQ 6.1.1 item purification may partially address this by removing poorly discriminating items. Sensitivity analyses comparing theta trajectories for participants with stable vs. shifting response styles could test robustness of two-phase pattern to response bias confounds.'"

**2. Repeated VR Testing Confidence Inflation**

- **Confound Description:** Repeated exposure to VR environment and test format may increase confidence through task familiarity independent of memory strength changes
- **How It Could Affect Results:** VR research shows practice effects on confidence: "participants who undergo VR interventions exhibited more confidence conducting tasks in real world" (systematic review, 2024). If test familiarity increases baseline confidence across sessions, this could MASK confidence decline (practice-driven increase compensates for memory-driven decrease), leading to underestimation of decay rates or flattened trajectories that fail to show two-phase pattern even if memory shows pattern.
- **Literature Evidence:** Neural plasticity research: "frequent training reinforces neural plasticity, strengthens synaptic connections" in VR contexts. Metacognitive confidence can improve through procedural learning (learning how to use confidence scale effectively) even as declarative memory (room details) decays.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** If practice effects are stronger in late retention intervals (cumulative learning across 4 tests), late-segment confidence slopes may be artificially shallow not because consolidation stabilized memory, but because practice effects compensate for decay. This creates spurious two-phase pattern.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 4: Analysis Approach - Discuss practice controls: 'Repeated VR testing may increase metacognitive confidence through task familiarity independent of memory strength. IRT theta scores partially control for this by modeling test session effects via item parameters, but additional controls could include: (1) test session as fixed effect covariate in LMM, (2) sensitivity analysis comparing participants with vs. without prior VR exposure (per methods.md demographics), (3) interaction term testing whether two-phase pattern strength varies by VR familiarity. If two-phase pattern replicates across familiarity levels, this suggests consolidation signal is robust to practice effects.'"
