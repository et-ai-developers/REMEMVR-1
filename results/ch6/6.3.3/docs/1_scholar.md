#### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**1. Overgeneralization of VR Ecological Encoding Eliminating Age Effects**

- **Location:** 1_concept.md - Section 2: Theoretical Background, paragraph "Theoretical Predictions"
- **Claim Made:** "VR ecological encoding creates age-invariant forgetting patterns that should extend to metacognitive judgments"
- **Scholarly Criticism:** While Chapter 5 found NULL age effects across multiple RQ types, this doesn't necessarily mean VR encoding universally eliminates age differences. Recent research shows VR age effects depend on encoding condition (intentional vs. incidental) and visual perspective (1PP vs. 3PP). Claiming VR "creates" age-invariant patterns oversimplifies task-dependent effects.
- **Counterevidence:** Sauzéon et al. (2023, *ScienceDirect*) found older adults showed less recollection of spatiotemporal context with intentional encoding (p<0.001) but similar recollection with incidental encoding in VR driving paradigm. Additionally, VR visual perspective effects differ by age: younger adults benefited from 1PP encoding while older adults showed no 1PP advantage.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** Revise claim to acknowledge task-dependency: "REMEMVR's specific VR encoding protocol (active manipulation, 1:1 movement mapping, multi-sensory integration) created age-invariant patterns in Chapter 5, though VR encoding parameters (intentional/incidental, perspective, interaction type) can modulate age effects. The current hypothesis tests whether this age-invariance extends to metacognitive monitoring."

#### Omission Errors (Missing Context or Claims)

**1. No Discussion of Overconfidence Bias in Older Adults' False Memories**

- **Missing Content:** Concept.md doesn't acknowledge that older adults show overconfidence specifically for false memories (lures) even when overall confidence calibration appears preserved
- **Why It Matters:** RQ 6.3.3 tests confidence trajectories, but if older adults are overconfident about errors (false memories for similar lures), this could inflate confidence estimates independently of accuracy. IRT theta scores for confidence may not capture this bias if it's item-type specific (correct vs. lure items).
- **Supporting Literature:** Shin et al. (2024, *Scientific Reports*) found older adults showed overconfidence in false memories for lures in mnemonic discrimination task (ROC analysis revealed false recollection), while confidence for completely new stimuli was calibrated. This pattern was NOT observed in younger adults. The study noted "older adults tend not to acknowledge some memory problems as a consequence of very high confidence in false memories."
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you account for age-related overconfidence in false memories (lures vs. targets)? Could this inflate older adults' confidence theta scores independently of actual memory accuracy, creating spurious age × domain interactions if lure distribution differs across What/Where/When?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background OR Section 7: Limitations - "Older adults may exhibit overconfidence specifically for false memories (lures) while maintaining calibrated confidence for correct responses (Shin et al., 2024). REMEMVR's recognition tests include visually/semantically similar foils. If foil difficulty differs across domains (e.g., spatial foils more distinctive than temporal foils), age-related overconfidence could create spurious domain interactions. Post-hoc analysis should examine confidence calibration separately for hits vs. false alarms across domains."

**2. Missing Context on Metacognitive Sensitivity vs. Bias Distinction**

- **Missing Content:** Concept.md predicts NULL age × domain × time interaction but doesn't specify whether this applies to metacognitive sensitivity (discrimination between correct/incorrect), metacognitive bias (overall confidence level), or both
- **Why It Matters:** Recent research shows age effects can dissociate for sensitivity vs. bias. Hertzog & Tauber (2021) found age-equivalent metacognitive efficiency (meta-d'/d' = sensitivity) despite age differences in memory performance, but this doesn't preclude age differences in overall confidence levels (bias). If older adults show lower baseline confidence (bias) but preserved discrimination (sensitivity), the Age main effect on intercept could emerge without Age × Time slope differences.
- **Supporting Literature:** Murphy et al. (2025, *Neuroscience of Consciousness*) demonstrated "complexity of metacognitive judgments" with dissociations between retrospective confidence (more bias-dependent) and feeling-of-knowing (more sensitivity-dependent) in older adults. Signal Detection Theory frameworks distinguish metacognitive sensitivity (distribution separation) from bias (criterion placement).
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "Does your hypothesis predict NULL age effects for both metacognitive sensitivity (ability to discriminate high vs. low confidence when correct vs. incorrect) AND metacognitive bias (overall confidence tendency)? IRT theta scores may primarily capture bias rather than sensitivity."
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background - "We distinguish metacognitive sensitivity (discrimination accuracy) from metacognitive bias (overall confidence tendency). Based on evidence for age-equivalent meta-d'/d' (Hertzog & Tauber, 2021), we predict NULL Age × Domain × Time for confidence trajectory slopes (sensitivity). However, Age main effect on intercept (bias) is possible if older adults show lower baseline confidence independently of discrimination ability. IRT theta scores primarily reflect overall confidence level (analogous to bias) rather than calibration accuracy (sensitivity)."

#### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Confidence-Accuracy Dissociation (Independence Hypothesis)**

- **Alternative Theory:** Confidence judgments may be generated independently from memory accuracy through separate cognitive processes, rather than confidence "tracking" accuracy as concept.md assumes
- **How It Applies:** If confidence relies on familiarity/fluency heuristics while accuracy depends on recollective detail retrieval, then confidence and accuracy could show different age × domain patterns. For example, older adults might maintain high confidence based on gist/familiarity even when losing recollective detail (accuracy).
- **Key Citation:** Fleming & Daw (2024, *Annual Review of Psychology*) argue "propositional confidence is informed by the observer's models of the world and their cognitive system, which may be more or less accurate—thus explaining why metacognitive judgments are inferential and sometimes diverge from task performance." Additionally, research on visual short-term memory shows "VSTM accuracy and confidence were dissociated" when cue conditions changed (accuracy impaired but confidence reduced regardless).
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** The core assumption—that confidence parallels accuracy—is testable but not guaranteed. If RQ 6.3.3 finds SIGNIFICANT Age × Domain interaction (while Ch5 accuracy showed NULL), this would support independence hypothesis rather than invalidate VR ecological encoding.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background - "While we predict confidence will parallel accuracy (NULL age × domain interaction replicating Ch5), confidence-accuracy dissociations are theoretically possible if metacognitive judgments rely on familiarity/fluency heuristics independently from recollective detail retrieval (Fleming & Daw, 2024). A significant Age × Domain × Time interaction would suggest metacognitive processes are differentially sensitive to domain forgetting compared to accuracy measures, supporting independence rather than coupled tracking."

#### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. VR Technology Proficiency as Age × Domain Moderator**

- **Confound Description:** Repeated VR testing across 4 sessions (Days 0, 1, 3, 6) may create practice effects that differ by age due to technology familiarity. Older adults with lower baseline VR proficiency may show accelerated confidence gains (through familiarization) that mask memory-based confidence decline, particularly for spatially-demanding Where domain.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If older adults gain VR navigation confidence across sessions while younger adults plateau, this could create spurious Age × Time interaction. More critically, if VR familiarization benefits spatial tasks (Where domain = more navigation-heavy) differentially from temporal tasks (When = less navigation-dependent), this could produce Age × Domain × Time interaction artifact.
- **Literature Evidence:** Frontiers in Human Neuroscience (2024) systematic review noted "technology-proficient individuals benefit from enhanced fine motor control, faster reaction times, and more adept navigation through on-screen interfaces. Such a confound is particularly problematic when testing older adults... who may lack consistent exposure to computers or gaming devices." The review found VR memory assessment confounds include "individual prior knowledge and experience, information amount, experiment time, usability of the system."
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** REMEMVR uses hand-tracking in 8×5m physical space across 4 rooms. Where domain tests require recalling spatial locations (pick-up/put-down), potentially more affected by VR proficiency than What (object identity) or When (temporal order). If older adults' spatial confidence improves via VR familiarization while object/temporal confidence remains stable, spurious Age × Domain × Time interaction could emerge.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** Add to Section 7: Limitations - "VR technology proficiency may confound Age × Domain interactions if repeated testing (4 sessions) differentially benefits spatial confidence in older adults with lower baseline VR familiarity. While REMEMVR provided 10-minute tutorials and used intuitive hand-tracking (no controllers), spatial tasks may be more sensitive to VR navigation confidence gains than object/temporal tasks. Future analyses could examine whether VR proficiency (measured via tutorial performance or self-reported gaming experience from demographics) moderates Age × Domain × Time effects."

**2. IRT Likert Response Bias Not Fully Addressed for Polytomous Models**

- **Confound Description:** Concept.md notes thesis/methods.md mentions "Likert response bias correction" but doesn't specify whether correction addresses IRT model assumptions for polytomous data. Recent research shows Likert item wording effects can bias IRT parameter estimates, particularly for graded response models (GRM) used in RQ 6.3.1.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If older adults use Likert scale differently (e.g., avoiding extreme categories, or showing acquiescence bias), and if bias correction doesn't account for this in GRM calibration, theta scores may reflect response style rather than true confidence. Critically, if response bias differs across domains (e.g., spatial questions elicit more extreme responses than temporal), Age × Domain interaction could be artifact of differential item functioning (DIF).
- **Literature Evidence:** Wang et al. (2015, cited in Frontiers in Psychology 2024) found "wording effect in international assessments was substantial. Ignoring the wording effect resulted in overestimated test reliability and biased person measures." Frontiers in Education (2021) showed GRM performance requires N≥300 for stable estimates and "missing data could impact performance of IRT models and lead to biased parameter estimates." Additionally, "positively worded items can better mitigate bias toward higher levels... negatively worded items possibly require more complex cognitive decision-making and might introduce semantic comprehension difficulties."
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** RQ 6.3.1 uses 5-category Likert confidence items (TC_* tags: 0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0) with GRM. If confidence wording differs across What/Where/When items (e.g., "How confident are you this is the correct object?" vs. "How confident are you this is the correct location?"), domain-specific wording effects could create DIF that manifests as Age × Domain interaction.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Mitigation:** Add to Section 4: Analysis Approach OR Section 7: Limitations - "While thesis/methods.md notes Likert response bias correction, we acknowledge GRM assumes consistent response style across items. If confidence item wording differs across What/Where/When domains, differential item functioning (DIF) could create spurious Age × Domain effects. Post-hoc DIF analysis should test whether item discrimination/difficulty parameters differ across domains for older vs. younger adults (Wang et al., 2015). Sample size (N=100) meets GRM minimum (N≥300 recommended for polytomous models per Frontiers in Education 2021), but parameter stability should be verified via bootstrap confidence intervals."
