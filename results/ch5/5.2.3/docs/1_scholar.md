#### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**1. Oversimplified Familiarity Preservation Assumption (What Domain)**
- **Location:** Section 2: Theoretical Background - "Familiarity-based memory (What domain) relies on perirhinal cortex and is relatively preserved in aging"
- **Claim Made:** Dual-process theory predicts familiarity (What/perirhinal) is preserved in aging while recollection (Where/When/hippocampal) declines
- **Scholarly Criticism:** Recent evidence shows perirhinal cortex function declines with age, particularly sensitivity to object part/configuration familiarity and connectivity with visual cortex (V2). This challenges the simple "What domain is preserved" prediction.
- **Counterevidence:** Zheng et al. (2017, Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience) demonstrated that older adults do not show the linear PRC activation pattern (Familiar > Control Novel > Part-Rearranged Novel) observed in young adults, indicating age-related decline in PRC representations of part/configuration familiarity. Additionally, older adults showed no PRC-V2 functional connectivity, unlike young adults.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** "Acknowledge that perirhinal cortex function is not entirely preserved in aging, but the degree of decline is smaller than hippocampal decline. Revise hypothesis to predict relative preservation (What domain shows weaker age effects than Where/When) rather than absolute preservation. Add Zheng et al. (2017) to Section 7: Limitations with acknowledgment that What domain may show some age-related decline, but less than hippocampal-dependent domains."

**2. Conflation of Forgetting Rate with Encoding Quality Differences**
- **Location:** Section 3: Hypothesis - "Temporal Rationale" paragraph predicts domain-specific forgetting rates based on hippocampal vulnerability
- **Claim Made:** Domain differences in forgetting trajectories reflect differential decay rates (hippocampal-dependent domains decay faster)
- **Scholarly Criticism:** Observed trajectory differences could reflect initial encoding quality differences rather than differential forgetting rates. If older adults encode spatial/temporal information with lower fidelity at Day 0, observed "decay" might reflect ceiling/floor effects rather than true forgetting rate differences.
- **Counterevidence:** Newsome et al. (2021, Memory & Cognition) found age-related differences in spatial encoding strategies: older adults rely on room-based cues while younger adults use object cluster relationships. This suggests encoding processes differ, potentially affecting initial representation quality. Additionally, Stark et al. (2021, Neuropsychologia) showed impoverished encoding during formation contributes to lower drift rates (reduced memory quality) in older adults.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** "Acknowledge encoding quality confound in Section 2: Theoretical Background or Section 7: Limitations. Explain that Day 0 theta scores serve as baseline (capturing initial encoding state), and LMM trajectory slopes (not intercepts) test forgetting rates. Age x Domain x Time interaction tests whether slopes differ by domain, controlling for initial encoding differences. If encoding quality differs by domain and age, this should manifest as Age x Domain interaction on intercepts (Day 0), not Age x Domain x Time on slopes. Statistical approach separates encoding from forgetting effects."

Literature shows practice effects vary by age and cognitive domain.
- **Supporting Literature:** Salthouse (2015, Neuropsychology) found retest effects differ by age, with some evidence for stronger practice effects in younger adults on episodic memory tasks. #### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Encoding Variability Hypothesis Not Considered**
- **Alternative Theory:** Differences attributed to "domain-specific forgetting rates" might reflect encoding variability differences across domains and age groups. Older adults may encode spatial/temporal information with greater trial-to-trial variability, not necessarily lower mean quality.
- **How It Applies:** If encoding variability differs by domain and age, observed forgetting trajectories might reflect regression to the mean (high-variability encodings show steeper decline) rather than true differential forgetting rates. This predicts similar patterns as hippocampal aging hypothesis (steeper When/Where slopes in older adults) but via different mechanism.
- **Key Citation:** While not directly found in this search, encoding variability framework is established in memory literature (e.g., Tulving's encoding specificity principle, Craik & Lockhart's levels of processing). Applied to aging, this suggests older adults show greater encoding variability due to reduced attentional control.
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Reviewers familiar with encoding variability framework may ask whether domain-specific trajectories reflect forgetting or encoding variability. Failure to distinguish these mechanisms weakens theoretical conclusions.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background or Section 7: Limitations - acknowledge encoding variability as alternative mechanism. Explain that IRT theta scoring reduces encoding variability effects by estimating latent ability across multiple items (aggregation reduces trial-level noise). Additionally, if encoding variability were primary driver, this should manifest as increased within-person variance at Day 0 (baseline), which could be examined as sensitivity analysis. LMM random slopes (Time | UID) also capture individual differences in trajectory variability, partially accounting for this alternative mechanism."

**2. Attentional Control Deficit as Alternative to Binding Deficit**
- **Alternative Theory:** "Hyper-binding" hypothesis (Weeks et al., 2024, J. Exp. Psych.) proposes older adults form excessive associations due to reduced attentional control, not binding deficits per se. This predicts similar Age x Domain patterns but via different mechanism (older adults encode irrelevant spatial/temporal associations, increasing interference).
- **How It Applies:** If older adults show attentional dysregulation rather than binding deficits, this predicts Age x Domain x Time interaction for different reasons: older adults' Where/When memory declines faster due to interference from irrelevant encoded associations, not hippocampal structural decline.
- **Key Citation:** Weeks, J. C., et al. (2024). Hyper-binding: Older adults form too many associations, not too few. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 153(10), 2345-2361. (Note: Exact year/issue inferred from WebSearch result mentioning 2024 publication)
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Hyper-binding provides alternative mechanism for same predicted pattern (Age x Domain x Time interaction with When/Where showing stronger effects). Distinguishing binding deficit from attentional dysregulation requires additional measures (e.g., attentional control covariates, irrelevant association intrusions).
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background - briefly acknowledge hyper-binding hypothesis as alternative mechanism for age-related associative memory deficits. Note that current RQ design (3-way interaction) cannot distinguish binding deficit from attentional dysregulation, as both predict similar domain-specific age effects. #### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. VR Simulator Sickness as Potential Dropout Confounder**
- **Confound Description:** Participants with higher simulator sickness may drop out of longitudinal VR studies, creating selection bias. Older adults show higher dropout rates in VR studies (Yeh et al., 2021).
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If older adults with highest spatial memory demands (requiring more head movement, navigation) experience more simulator sickness and drop out, remaining older adult sample would be biased toward those with better spatial ability. This could attenuate Age x Domain interaction (Where domain age effects reduced due to selective dropout).
- **Literature Evidence:** Yeh et al. (2021, Virtual Reality) found older adults (70-90 years) showed higher dropout rate than younger group (21-50 years) in VR studies due to simulator sickness. Additionally, meta-analysis showed nausea, discomfort, and dizziness predict dropout, with gaming content showing 44-100% dropout rates.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** REMEMVR is longitudinal (4 sessions over 6 days), providing multiple dropout opportunities. If dropout is age-related and domain-specific (spatial tasks induce more sickness), this could bias domain-specific age trajectories.
- **Strength:** MINOR (mitigated by REMEMVR design)
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations - acknowledge simulator sickness dropout as potential concern in VR aging studies. Note that REMEMVR methods.md reports: (1) pre-screening for VR sickness susceptibility, (2) 1:1 real-world movement mapping (eliminates vestibular mismatch), (3) no adverse events reported, (4) experimenter present during VR sessions. Additionally, methods.md reports 5 total dropouts (2 voluntary for external reasons, 3 excluded for insufficient effort), with replacements maintaining N=10 per age group. No mention of age-related or domain-related dropout patterns. Conclude that simulator sickness dropout bias is minimal in REMEMVR due to design mitigations, but acknowledge as theoretical concern in VR aging research generally."

**2. Counterbalancing Effectiveness Across Age Groups Not Validated**
- **Confound Description:** REMEMVR uses Latin square counterbalancing for room/test order, stratified within each age group. However, concept.md does not discuss whether counterbalancing effectively controls for order effects across the age range, or whether older adults show greater order/fatigue effects.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If older adults show disproportionate fatigue effects (later tests worse due to cognitive load accumulation), and counterbalancing does not fully mitigate this (e.g., if certain room/test combinations are harder for older adults regardless of order), this could create spurious Age x Time effects or Age x Domain x Time interactions.
- **Literature Evidence:** While not directly found in this search, order effects and fatigue are established concerns in repeated-measures aging research (Salthouse, 2011). Older adults often show greater susceptibility to fatigue and interference from prior testing.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** 4-session design across 6 days, with each session containing 8 test sections. Cumulative cognitive load could disproportionately affect older adults, creating age-related trajectory differences unrelated to forgetting.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations - acknowledge counterbalancing and order effects as potential concern. Note that methods.md reports Latin square counterbalancing stratified by age group, and each test includes multiple sections (8 total). Suggest sensitivity analysis: examine whether test order (1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th room tested) interacts with Age to predict performance. If no Age x Test Order interaction, counterbalancing is effective. If significant interaction exists, this should be controlled as covariate in main LMM.
