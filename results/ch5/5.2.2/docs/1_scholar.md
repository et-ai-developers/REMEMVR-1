### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**1. Oversimplified Perirhinal vs Hippocampal Distinction**
- **Location:** 1_concept.md - Section 2: Theoretical Background, Hippocampal replay theories claim
- **Claim Made:** "What memory can rely more on perirhinal cortex and familiarity processes"
- **Scholarly Criticism:** The perirhinal-hippocampal distinction is more nuanced than implied. Recent evidence shows perirhinal cortex involvement varies with task demands and delay periods, not just memory type.
- **Counterevidence:** Frontiers (2020, Mol Neurosci) showed CA1 required for object recognition at >20 min delays, while perirhinal supports shorter delays - suggesting the distinction is delay-dependent, not purely domain-dependent
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** Acknowledge that perirhinal vs hippocampal involvement depends on retention interval as well as memory domain. The 24-hour consolidation window tested here likely engages hippocampal systems for all domains, making the consolidation benefit comparison still valid.

**2. Temporal Memory Consolidation Incompletely Theorized**
- **Location:** 1_concept.md - Section 3: Hypothesis, primary hypothesis
- **Claim Made:** Hypothesis focuses on Where vs What consolidation but When memory is included without equivalent theoretical rationale
- **Scholarly Criticism:** The concept does not adequately theorize why When (temporal order) memory might or might not benefit from sleep consolidation
- **Counterevidence:** PLOS ONE research shows sleep specifically enforces temporal order in memory by strengthening forward associations - temporal memory IS hippocampus-dependent and DOES benefit from sleep
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** Add explicit theorizing about When memory: "Temporal sequence memory is hippocampus-dependent (PLOS ONE, 2007) and sleep strengthens forward temporal associations. Therefore, When may show consolidation benefits similar to Where, though the neural mechanisms may differ."

### Omission Errors (Missing Context or Claims)

**1. No Discussion of Practice Effects in Repeated VR Testing**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that participants complete memory tests 4 times (Days 0, 1, 3, 6), which could confound forgetting curves
- **Why It Matters:** Practice effects could mask memory decay - improvements from repeated testing might offset forgetting
- **Supporting Literature:** PMC Practice Effects Review states practice effects are "large, pervasive, and underappreciated" in longitudinal cognitive studies; average gains on repeat administration often exceed normative cognitive change
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you distinguish genuine consolidation/decay effects from practice-related improvements across the 4 test sessions?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** Add to Analysis Approach or create new Limitations section: "Practice effects from repeated testing (4 sessions) may influence observed trajectories. However, IRT theta scoring separates item difficulty from ability, and the piecewise model tests SLOPE differences rather than absolute levels, reducing practice effect confounding."

**2. No Acknowledgment of Encoding Quality Differences Across Domains**
- **Missing Content:** The concept attributes trajectory differences to consolidation/decay, but initial encoding quality may differ across domains
- **Why It Matters:** If spatial information is encoded more richly during VR experience, observed trajectories might reflect ceiling effects rather than differential forgetting
- **Supporting Literature:** VR research shows encoding of visual-spatial information is "more cerebrally challenging" than retrieval; physical walking enhances spatial encoding quality
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you know domain differences in trajectories reflect differential consolidation rather than differential encoding quality?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** Add to Theoretical Background: "Day 0 serves as baseline capturing initial encoding state. The analysis focuses on trajectory SLOPES (rate of change) rather than intercepts (initial levels), allowing assessment of consolidation/decay processes independent of encoding quality differences."

**3. Sleep Data Not Integrated into Analysis**
- **Missing Content:** Methods.md shows sleep hygiene data collected at each test point, but concept.md doesn't mention incorporating this into the consolidation analysis
- **Why It Matters:** If sleep-dependent consolidation is the mechanism, actual sleep data should be considered
- **Supporting Literature:** Sawangjit et al. (2020) showed deeper sleep (more SWS) specifically predicts better spatial memory persistence
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "You claim sleep consolidation is the mechanism, but did you measure or control for actual sleep quality between Day 0 and Day 1?"
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Addition:** Consider adding sleep quality as exploratory covariate in LMM, or acknowledge in Discussion that sleep data is available for follow-up analyses

### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Multiple Trace Theory vs Systems Consolidation**
- **Alternative Theory:** Multiple Trace Theory (Nadel & Moscovitch) holds that hippocampal-dependent memories REMAIN hippocampus-dependent, challenging the systems consolidation view
- **How It Applies:** Under MTT, spatial memories might stay hippocampus-dependent longer, not because of consolidation advantage but because they never transfer to neocortex
- **Key Citation:** Moscovitch et al. (2016, Annual Review of Psychology) - Episodic memory and beyond
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Reviewers familiar with MTT will ask how predictions differ from systems consolidation
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** The piecewise model tests consolidation RATE (slopes), not whether memories ever become hippocampus-independent. Both MTT and systems consolidation predict initial hippocampal involvement; the current design tests short-term consolidation dynamics compatible with both frameworks.

**2. Contextual Binding Theory**
- **Alternative Theory:** CB theory proposes hippocampus permanently supports recollection while neocortex supports familiarity/semantic memory - different from systems consolidation's temporary hippocampal role
- **How It Applies:** Domain differences might reflect recollection vs familiarity demands rather than consolidation effects
- **Key Citation:** Yonelinas et al. (2019, Nature Reviews Neuroscience) - A contextual binding theory of episodic memory
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** CB theory is a major alternative framework for hippocampal memory function
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** Brief mention that results will be interpreted primarily through systems consolidation lens, with CB theory as alternative interpretive framework for discussion.

### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. Test-Retest Reliability and Practice Effects**
- **Confound Description:** Four testing sessions may produce practice effects that confound forgetting curves
- **How It Could Affect Results:** Improvements from test familiarity could mask decay, potentially differentially across domains
- **Literature Evidence:** PMC meta-analysis shows g = 0.28 retest effect from first to second administration, increasing through fourth administration
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** 4-session design (Days 0, 1, 3, 6) provides multiple practice opportunities
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** Note in Analysis Approach that IRT separates item parameters from ability estimates, and piecewise model tests slope differences (resistant to practice effects affecting levels). REMEMVR uses different rooms for each test day (counterbalanced), reducing direct item repetition.

**2. Room/Item Counterbalancing Effects**
- **Confound Description:** Different rooms tested at different delays - room difficulty differences could confound domain comparisons
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If Room A has harder spatial items than Room B, and they're tested at different delays, this confounds delay effects
- **Literature Evidence:** General psychometric principle - counterbalancing controls for systematic effects but increases variance
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** Latin square counterbalancing described in methods.md
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Mitigation:** Acknowledge counterbalancing in Analysis Approach. Consider including Room as random effect or note that aggregation across participants averages out room-specific effects.
