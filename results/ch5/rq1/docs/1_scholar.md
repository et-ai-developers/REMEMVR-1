#### Omission Errors (Missing Context or Claims)

**1. No Discussion of Practice Effects in Repeated Testing**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that participants complete the same VR test 4 times (Days 0, 1, 3, 6), which could generate practice effects that confound forgetting curve interpretation
- **Why It Matters:** Practice effects are characteristic of nearly all cognitive tasks when repeated during serial assessments (Calamia et al., 2013). Effect sizes of d = 0.25 are typical for cognitive composites across 3 assessments over 6-12 months. Positive retest effects can obscure genuine memory decline or mask domain-specific decay patterns. For longitudinal memory studies, practice-related improvements might counteract forgetting-related decline, biasing trajectory estimates.
- **Supporting Literature:** Calamia et al. (2013, *The Clinical Neuropsychologist*) found practice effects persist across multiple assessments with d = 0.25 for composite measures. Sliwinski et al. (2018, *Psychological Aging*) demonstrated that substantial positive retest effects can obscure stability in memory performance. In preclinical Alzheimer's research, lack of practice effects (not actual decline) is often the first divergence from healthy trajectories, indicating practice effects are theoretically meaningful signals.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you distinguish genuine memory decay from practice-related improvements masking decay? Could domain differences reflect differential practice effects rather than differential forgetting rates?"
- **Strength:** CRITICAL
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 4: Analysis Strategy OR create new Section 7: Limitations - Acknowledge practice effects are likely present in 4-session design. Discuss two mitigating factors: (1) IRT theta ability estimates separate item difficulty from person ability, partially accounting for task familiarity effects; (2) Non-linear trajectory models (logarithmic, quadratic) can capture initial practice-driven improvements vs. later decay-driven decline. Note that Domain × Time interaction tests whether practice effects differ across domains - if practice is uniform, interaction would be null even with practice present. Consider including test session as covariate in LMM sensitivity analysis."

**2. Encoding Quality Differences as Alternative to Decay Differences**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md attributes domain differences to "forgetting rates" but doesn't acknowledge that observed trajectory differences might reflect initial encoding quality variations rather than (or in addition to) differential decay rates
- **Why It Matters:** If spatial information is encoded more richly during initial VR experience (greater hippocampal engagement), observed "decay trajectories" might reflect ceiling effects (spatial starts higher) or floor effects (temporal starts lower) rather than true differential forgetting. Encoding-retrieval similarity research shows that memory quality depends on reinstatement of encoding states - domain differences could emerge from encoding rather than consolidation/retrieval stages.
- **Supporting Literature:** Bonnici et al. (2018, *Journal of Neuroscience*) showed spatial context encoded with greater hippocampal engagement than temporal context in autobiographical memory tasks, suggesting initial encoding differences precede any decay. Palombo et al. (2020, *Alzheimer's Research & Therapy*) demonstrated that encoding failure vs. retrieval failure have distinct neural substrates (hippocampal for encoding, frontal for retrieval), complicating interpretation of domain differences as purely decay-related.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you rule out that domain differences reflect encoding quality variations rather than forgetting rate differences? Could What items be easier to encode initially, creating apparent resilience that's not about decay?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background - Acknowledge that domain differences could reflect encoding quality variations, not just decay rates. Explain why Day 0 (T1) serves as baseline capturing initial encoding state - longitudinal trajectory slopes (not intercepts) test forgetting dynamics. If encoding quality drives domain differences, we'd expect domain differences at T1 but parallel trajectories; if forgetting drives differences, we'd expect Domain × Time interaction. The interaction term statistically isolates decay dynamics from initial encoding. Consider adding T1 domain comparison as preliminary analysis to descriptively characterize initial encoding differences."

**3. VR Simulator Sickness as Potential Dropout Confounder**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not mention that VR simulator sickness could cause non-random dropout, potentially biasing domain-specific trajectories if certain memory domains (e.g., spatial navigation) induce more sickness
- **Why It Matters:** Stangl et al. (2023, *Frontiers in Virtual Reality*) found 33% dropout in high-workload VR conditions vs. 10% in low-workload conditions, with dropout not missing at random but bound to previous symptom ratings. If spatial memory tasks (navigation-heavy) induce more cybersickness, differential dropout could bias spatial domain trajectories. Gaming VR content shows 44-100% dropout rates in some studies. Cybersickness negatively affects visuospatial working memory and psychomotor skills during task performance.
- **Mitigation in REMEMVR Study:** Methods.md reports: (1) Pre-screening for VR sickness susceptibility, (2) 1:1 real-world movement (no artificial locomotion), (3) Experimenter supervision, (4) No participants reported adverse events. Five participants excluded during data collection (2 withdrew for personal reasons, 3 excluded for insufficient effort) but replaced to maintain N=100. No evidence of VR sickness-driven dropout in this study.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "Could VR simulator sickness cause differential dropout across domains, biasing trajectory estimates?"
- **Strength:** MINOR (given methods.md reports no adverse events, but worth acknowledging)
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations - Acknowledge VR simulator sickness is a known confounder in longitudinal VR studies (Stangl et al., 2023), with potential for non-random dropout. Note that REMEMVR mitigated this through: (1) pre-screening, (2) 1:1 movement mapping, (3) supervision. Report that no participants reported adverse events and dropout (N=5) was not VR sickness-related. Conclude that VR sickness is unlikely to confound domain trajectories in this dataset."

**4. Test-Retest Interference and Consolidation Effects**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not discuss whether repeated retrieval attempts (testing effect) might interact with consolidation processes differently across memory domains
- **Why It Matters:** Repeated testing strengthens memory via retrieval-induced consolidation (testing effect). However, retrieval-induced forgetting also occurs for non-retrieved competitors. If What/Where/When domains differ in susceptibility to retrieval-induced strengthening vs. interference, observed trajectories reflect combined effects of: (1) natural forgetting, (2) retrieval-induced strengthening, (3) retrieval-induced interference. Research shows retrieval is a "fast route to consolidation," meaning repeated testing on Days 0, 1, 3, 6 might consolidate some domain information more than others.
- **Supporting Literature:** Research on retest effects shows beneficial effects of testing on target memory are most evident after long delays, suggesting consolidation mechanisms. Retest effects in working memory show d = 0.28 from first to second test, with significant increases up to fourth test administration (meta-analysis by Scharfen et al., 2018). Test-retest interval length moderates effect size.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "Could domain differences reflect differential susceptibility to retrieval-induced consolidation rather than natural forgetting? Are you measuring decay or testing effects?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations - Acknowledge that repeated testing likely induces retrieval-based consolidation, confounding natural forgetting trajectories. Note that Domain × Time interaction tests whether this confound differs across domains - if retrieval strengthening is uniform, interaction would be null. IRT modeling partially addresses this by separating item difficulty (which captures item-level retrieval strength) from person ability (which captures memory state). Consider framing RQ as testing 'memory trajectory under repeated retrieval' rather than 'pure forgetting' to acknowledge testing-effect reality."

#### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Encoding-Retrieval Similarity Framework**
- **Alternative Theory:** Encoding-retrieval similarity accounts propose that memory quality depends on reinstatement of encoding-time neural states during retrieval, mediated by hippocampal interactions (Xue et al., 2013, *Cerebral Cortex*). Domain differences might reflect how well VR test context reinstates original encoding context for each domain.
- **How It Applies:** What (object identity) might show better encoding-retrieval overlap because objects are visually re-presented during recognition tests, whereas Where (spatial layout) requires mental reconstruction without full environmental re-immersion. When (temporal order) requires inferential reconstruction with minimal external cues. Domain differences could reflect quality of contextual reinstatement, not decay rates.
- **Key Citation:** Xue et al. (2013) demonstrated that hippocampus mediates the link between cortical encoding-retrieval similarity and memory success. Greater neural similarity between encoding and retrieval states predicts better memory.
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** This alternative predicts the same empirical pattern (What > Where > When) but through retrieval mechanisms rather than consolidation/decay mechanisms. Without acknowledging this, reviewers might question whether findings support dual-process theory or simply reflect test design (visual recognition for What, reconstruction for Where/When).
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add to Section 2: Theoretical Background or Section 7: Limitations - Acknowledge encoding-retrieval similarity as alternative framework. Explain that What items receive visual recognition probes (high contextual reinstatement), while Where/When require reconstruction (low reinstatement). Note that dual-process theory and encoding-retrieval similarity are not mutually exclusive - familiarity-based retrieval might succeed when contextual reinstatement is partial, whereas recollection requires full reinstatement. Both frameworks predict What > Where > When, making them difficult to dissociate with current design."

**2. Differential Consolidation Windows (Not Uniform Sleep Benefit)**
- **Alternative Theory:** Standard consolidation theory (cited in concept.md) predicts hippocampal-dependent memories benefit more from sleep-dependent consolidation. However, recent research shows consolidation windows differ by content type - some memories consolidate rapidly (hours), others slowly (days). Domain differences might reflect different consolidation timescales rather than overall vulnerability.
- **How It Applies:** What (object identity) might consolidate rapidly in initial 24 hours (Day 0 → Day 1), showing stability by Day 3. Where (spatial layout) might consolidate gradually across Days 1-3, showing late stabilization. When (temporal order) might show no consolidation benefit, exhibiting continuous decay. This predicts Domain × Time interaction but through differentiated consolidation dynamics, not uniform decay.
- **Key Citation:** Rasch & Born (2013) note that consolidation operates on multiple timescales, but concept.md doesn't explore how this might differentiate across domains.
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Non-linear trajectory models (logarithmic, quadratic) could capture differentiated consolidation windows, but hypothesis doesn't articulate this. Missing theoretical specificity about which domains consolidate when (vs. generic "hippocampal memories more vulnerable").
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add to Section 3: Hypothesis, Secondary Hypotheses - Consider specifying consolidation window predictions: 'What consolidates rapidly (Days 0-1), Where consolidates gradually (Days 1-3), When shows no consolidation benefit.' This provides more specific theoretical predictions for non-linear model parameters (e.g., logarithmic model should show rapid early decay for What, delayed decay for Where, linear decay for When). Adds theoretical precision to non-linearity prediction."

#### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. Confidence Rating Response Biases Interact with Domain**
- **Confound Description:** Methods.md reports confidence ratings on 5-point Likert scale (1=Guess, 5=Absolutely Certain) with Likert response bias correction applied. However, concept.md focuses exclusively on accuracy (dichotomized TQ_* items) without discussing whether confidence-accuracy calibration differs across domains.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If participants are systematically overconfident for What (familiar objects) but underconfident for Where/When (requiring effortful recollection), confidence-weighted accuracy measures might show different trajectories than raw accuracy. Research on dual-process theory shows familiarity-based decisions have different confidence distributions than recollection-based decisions (Yonelinas, 2002). Domain differences in confidence calibration could confound interpretation of forgetting trajectories if confidence is later analyzed.
- **Literature Evidence:** Dual-process models predict familiarity generates graded confidence (inverted U-shape distribution), while recollection generates high-threshold confidence (bimodal distribution). If confidence trajectories differ from accuracy trajectories across domains, this supports dual-process account but requires separate analysis.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** Concept.md specifies dichotomous accuracy only (TQ_* items: <1 → 0, >=1 → 1), ignoring confidence. While appropriate for IRT-based RQ 5.1, confidence data exist and domain differences in confidence calibration would provide converging evidence for dual-process theory. Missing opportunity for triangulation.
- **Strength:** MINOR (not applicable to current RQ scope, but worth noting)
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 7: Limitations or future directions - Note that confidence ratings are collected but not analyzed in RQ 5.1 (accuracy-only focus). Acknowledge that domain differences in confidence calibration (familiarity = graded confidence, recollection = threshold confidence) would provide converging evidence for dual-process theory and could be examined in future RQs. Methods.md reports Likert response bias correction was applied, ensuring confidence data quality for future analysis."

**2. Counterbalancing of Room-Test Pairing Across Participants**
- **Confound Description:** Methods.md reports Latin square counterbalancing of room order and test order. However, concept.md does not discuss whether room-specific memory characteristics (e.g., bathroom more memorable than kitchen) could interact with test timing to confound domain trajectories.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If certain rooms have inherently more distinctive What items (e.g., bathroom: toothbrush is highly room-congruent, strong encoding) vs. less distinctive Where layouts (generic rectangular room), room-level variability could inflate domain differences. Counterbalancing distributes this across test sessions, but if room effects are large, they add noise to domain estimates.
- **Literature Evidence:** Methods.md describes strict design rules to equate rooms (equal memorability), but empirical verification is not mentioned. Room-level item characteristics (congruent, incongruent, common objects) are balanced, but perceptual salience (e.g., bathroom lighting, bedroom clock prominence) might vary.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** IRT calibration estimates item difficulty (b parameter) which captures item-level memorability. Extreme items (|b| > 3.0) are purified in 2-pass IRT. This methodological choice partially mitigates room-level confounds by removing outlier items. However, concept.md doesn't explicitly connect purification to room-level variance control.
- **Strength:** MINOR (2-pass IRT purification already addresses this, but not explicitly stated as rationale)
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "Add to Section 4: Analysis Strategy, Step 5 (Item Purification) - Clarify that 2-pass IRT purification serves dual purpose: (1) removes psychometrically problematic items (low discrimination, extreme difficulty), and (2) controls for room-level memorability variance by excluding outlier items. Note that Latin square counterbalancing distributes room-test pairing across participants, preventing systematic confounding of room characteristics with test timing. IRT item parameters capture room-level effects, which are then standardized out in theta ability estimates."

**3. Age-Related Differences in Domain-Specific Memory Across 20-70 Age Range**
