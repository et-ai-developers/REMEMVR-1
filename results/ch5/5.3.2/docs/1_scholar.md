#### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**1. Assumption of Equal Measurement Precision Across Paradigms**
- **Location:** 1_concept.md - Section: Hypothesis, "Expected Effect Pattern"
- **Claim Made:** "Slope estimates (Day 3): Free ~ -0.08, Cued ~ -0.06, Recognition ~ -0.04"
- **Scholarly Criticism:** Assumes slopes can be compared directly across paradigms without accounting for differential measurement error. Recognition typically has lower variance (ceiling effects) which can attenuate slope estimates regardless of true forgetting rate.
- **Counterevidence:** Hockley et al. (2024, Mem Cogn) demonstrated that ceiling and floor effects distort slopes of forgetting curves and limit detection of true differences between conditions.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** IRT theta scoring (used in RQ5.3) already accounts for item difficulty, producing ability estimates on common scale. Acknowledge that ceiling effects remain a concern for recognition paradigm and interpret attenuated recognition slopes cautiously.

---

#### Omission Errors (Missing Context or Claims)

**1. No Discussion of Ceiling Effects in Recognition**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that recognition memory typically shows near-perfect performance, especially at short delays, which can artificially flatten forgetting slopes.
- **Why It Matters:** Recognition paradigm may show slower "forgetting" not because retrieval support preserves memories, but because ceiling effects prevent detection of true decay.
- **Supporting Literature:** Smith et al. (2021) found ~90% accuracy in VR recognition tests even at longer delays, indicating ceiling effects. Hockley et al. (2024) explicitly warned that ceiling effects distort forgetting rate estimates.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you distinguish genuine preservation of memory (due to retrieval support) from artifactual floor in forgetting rate (due to ceiling effects)?"
- **Strength:** CRITICAL
- **Suggested Addition:** Add to Section: Analysis Approach or new Limitations section - acknowledge ceiling effect concern, note that IRT theta scoring partially addresses this by estimating ability on latent scale rather than raw accuracy, but residual ceiling effects may still attenuate recognition slopes.

**2. No Discussion of Practice Effects Across Repeated Testing**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md does not acknowledge that participants complete memory tests 4 times (Days 0, 1, 3, 6), potentially introducing practice-related improvements that confound forgetting trajectories.
- **Why It Matters:** Practice effects are "large, pervasive, and underappreciated" (Gavelin et al., 2022) and may differentially affect paradigms. If recognition benefits more from practice (easier cue recognition), this could mask forgetting and artificially reduce recognition slopes.
- **Supporting Literature:** Gavelin et al. (2022, Front Aging Neurosci) found practice effects persist across repeated cognitive testing, especially in memory domains. Reuter-Lorenz (2016) characterized practice effects as "a critically important aspect of studies of cognitive change."
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "Could differential practice effects across paradigms explain the predicted slope ordering, rather than retrieval support?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** Add to Analysis Approach - acknowledge practice effects as potential confound. Note that linear/logarithmic trajectories modeled in RQ5.3 capture non-linear change patterns that distinguish decay from practice improvement. Alternatively, discuss in thesis methods chapter.

**3. No Acknowledgment of Cue Quality Variability**
- **Missing Content:** Concept.md treats retrieval support as a simple continuum without acknowledging that cue effectiveness varies based on encoding-cue overlap (Tulving's encoding specificity principle).
- **Why It Matters:** "Cued recall" is not a homogeneous category - effectiveness depends on cue-target associative strength. Weak cues may produce recall worse than free recall (documented by Roediger & Payne, 1983).
- **Supporting Literature:** Nairne argues that cue distinctiveness, not encoding-retrieval match per se, determines retrieval success. This alternative account could explain paradigm differences without invoking "retrieval support."
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you control for cue quality in cued recall? Could differential cue-target associations explain results?"
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Addition:** Brief acknowledgment in Theoretical Background that cue quality in REMEMVR cued recall tasks is controlled by design (standardized cues across items), partially addressing this concern.

#### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Cue Distinctiveness vs. Retrieval Support**
- **Alternative Theory:** Nairne's cue distinctiveness theory proposes that what determines successful retrieval is not encoding-retrieval match (Tulving) but cue distinctiveness - how well a cue discriminates the target from competitors.
- **How It Applies:** Paradigm differences might reflect differential cue distinctiveness rather than "support." Recognition provides distinctive cues (the target itself), while free recall provides only generic context cues.
- **Key Citation:** Nairne (various works, Purdue University) - primary opponent of encoding specificity principle
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Reviewers familiar with cue distinctiveness debate may question whether "retrieval support" is the correct theoretical framing.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** Optional mention in Theoretical Background that cue distinctiveness offers alternative explanation; note that both theories predict same ordinal pattern for paradigm comparison.

**2. Item Difficulty Confound**
- **Alternative Theory:** Observed paradigm differences in forgetting rates might reflect item difficulty differences rather than retrieval support. If recognition items are inherently easier (target + foils easier than generation), initial encoding quality may differ.
- **How It Applies:** The 3-paradigm comparison assumes items are equivalent except for retrieval demands. But if recognition items require less elaborative encoding initially, observed "slower forgetting" reflects shallower encoding not better retrieval.
- **Key Citation:** Jacoby (1974), cited in desirable difficulty literature, warned that "one must be concerned with differences in item difficulty that confound condition difficulty."
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** IRT theta scoring addresses this by estimating person ability independent of item difficulty, but worth acknowledging.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** Note in Analysis Approach that IRT theta scoring controls for item difficulty by separating person ability from item parameters.

#### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. Three-Point Curve Fitting Limitations**
- **Confound Description:** Linear trend test using only 3 data points (3 paradigms) has limited power to detect non-linearity and high sensitivity to any single outlier paradigm.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** Even if linear trend is significant, R-squared of 3-point regression is mechanically high and may not indicate true linearity. A single deviant paradigm could produce misleading conclusions.
- **Literature Evidence:** Standard statistical guidance notes that 3-point regressions have limited interpretability for curve shape inference.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** The R-squared > 0.90 criterion may be easily satisfied even with poor theoretical fit.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** Acknowledge in Analysis Approach that 3-point linear trend has inherent limitations. Consider supplementing with exact slope ordering test (is Free > Cued AND Cued > Recognition?) rather than relying solely on R-squared.

**2. Dependency on RQ5.3 Model Specification**
- **Confound Description:** RQ5.4 uses slope estimates from RQ5.3's best-fitting model. If that model is misspecified (e.g., wrong functional form), extracted slopes will be biased.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** Linear trend test operates on potentially biased slope estimates rather than raw data.
- **Literature Evidence:** Derived parameter estimates inherit uncertainty from parent model - standard hierarchical modeling concern.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** RQ5.4 is entirely dependent on RQ5.3 output quality.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Mitigation:** Concept.md correctly specifies this dependency. Suggest adding confidence intervals on slope estimates to propagate uncertainty to trend test.
