### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**[1] Schema Compensation Assumed Without Acknowledging Interference Alternative**

- **Location:** 1_concept.md - Section 2 (Theoretical Background), paragraph 2
- **Claim Made:** "Older adults may rely more heavily on schema-based processing to compensate for declining hippocampal function" and "Congruent items benefit from this strategy, showing attenuated age effects. Incongruent items cannot leverage schema support, showing maximal age-related vulnerability."
- **Scholarly Criticism:** The concept.md assumes schemas are protective but does not acknowledge that recent literature (Nasreddine et al., 2024, *Cognition*) documents TWO competing accounts: compensation (schemas fill gaps) vs. interference (schemas actively bias memory). If older adults perform worse on incongruent items, it could be because: (A) schemas don't protect incongruent items (gap-filling account), OR (B) schemas actively interfere with incongruent memory through inhibitory bias (interference account). The RQ design does not explicitly test between these mechanisms.

- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** Add to Section 2: "Two theoretical accounts explain age-related schema effects. The compensatory (gap-filling) account proposes that schemas help older adults when memory fails. The interference account proposes that schemas actively bias older adults away from incongruent information. This study tests the gradient prediction (incongruent > common > congruent age effects) which is consistent with compensation, but does not directly isolate mechanisms. If Age × Congruence interaction emerges primarily at post-Day 0 timepoints (not intercepts), this would favor forgetting moderation over encoding differences and support the compensatory mechanism."

### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**[1] Schema Interference vs. Compensation Debate**

- **Alternative Theory:** The schema interference account (Nasreddine et al., 2024, *Cognition*) proposes that age-related schema effects are NOT primarily compensatory but reflect age-related inhibitory control deficits. By this account, older adults' underlying memory for incongruent information is largely intact, but schema knowledge actively INTERFERES with correct responding by biasing them toward schema-consistent errors.
- **How It Applies:** If this RQ finds that older adults show worse memory for incongruent items, it could be explained by: (A) Compensation: older adults use schemas less for incongruent items (Gap account), OR (B) Interference: schemas actively bias older adults toward schema-consistent errors for incongruent items (Interference account). Both produce the same empirical pattern (Age × Incongruent), but have different mechanisms and implications.
- **Key Citation:** Nasreddine et al. (2024) found that age effects on schema bias were partially (but not fully) mediated by memory failures, suggesting both mechanisms operate. The paper states: "There are two major accounts of this effect: One proposes that schemas compensate for memory failures in aging, and the other proposes that schemas instead actively impair older adults' otherwise intact memory for incongruent information."
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Reviewers will expect discussion of how the study design tests between compensation and interference accounts. If only main effects or simple ordering is found (incongruent > common > congruent), both theories predict that. To distinguish accounts, the concept could propose examining whether Age × Congruence effects emerge in recollection vs. familiarity (compensation predicts recollection deficits; interference predicts both), or whether error types differ (false positives from schema intrusions vs. omissions from memory failure).
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** Add to Section 2 (Theoretical Background): "Two theoretical accounts explain how schemas affect memory in aging. The compensatory account proposes schemas support memory when recollection fails, predicting better memory for congruent items in older adults. The interference account proposes schemas actively bias older adults toward schema-consistent errors, predicting systematic false positives for incongruent items. Both accounts predict an Age × Congruence interaction with this RQ's predicted pattern. Future research could distinguish mechanisms by examining error types (intrusions vs. omissions) or signal detection parameters (hit rates vs. false alarm rates). The current study tests whether the predicted gradient emerges and interprets via consolidation mechanisms, leaving mechanism specification to future work."

**[2] Sleep-Dependent Consolidation Variability by Age and Engagement**

- **Alternative Theory:** Recent literature (2020-2024) on sleep-dependent memory consolidation suggests that age-related consolidation deficits may be REVERSED if material is personally engaging. The alternative framework proposes that age differences in forgetting curves depend critically on motivation/engagement during retention intervals, not just biological consolidation capacity.
- **How It Applies:** The REMEMVR task is VR-based and novel (high engagement potential). WebSearch found: "Declarative memory consolidation may be preserved in older adults if they perceive study material to be engaging and personally relevant" and "sleep continues to enhance episodic memory in aging especially when the to-be-remembered material engages interests close to the daily life of older adults." If older adults in REMEMVR find the VR task more engaging than younger participants (or vice versa), consolidation efficiency could differ, confounding the age × schema effects.
- **Key Citation:** Sleep consolidation meta-analysis (2019-2023) found "memory retention was better after a night of sleep compared with wakefulness, regardless of delay (few hours vs. 12+ hours), but the sleep effect was modest" and "engagement and relevance" factors modulate consolidation in aging.
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** The 4-day retention interval (Day 0 → Day 1/3/6) includes multiple sleep periods. If older and younger adults differ in VR task engagement (subjective experience), this could affect consolidation efficiency independent of schema effects. The concept could acknowledge this by measuring engagement or noting that VR novelty may differentially engage older adults.

### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**[1] Test-Retest Practice Effects Across 4-Session Design**

- **Confound Description:** Participants complete 4 successive VR memory tests across 6 days, with each test targeting one of the four rooms (Latin square counterbalancing). VR memory research documents significant practice/learning effects between sessions 1-2, with ICC values 0.618-0.922 (wide range suggesting heterogeneous practice curves).
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If older and younger adults have different learning curves across sessions (e.g., older adults improve more slowly from Day 0 → Day 1), raw forgetting curves could be confounded with practice effects. For example, if older adults show flatter improvement curves, Day 1 performance might appear lower (worse memory) when actually it reflects slower practice acquisition. Conversely, if older adults "catch up" on practice by Day 3, this could mask true age-related forgetting.
- **Literature Evidence:** VR memory assessment review (2023, *Frontiers Human Neuroscience*) found "learning effects evident between sessions 1 and 2 for 7 of 8 mean measures" and "initial training recommended to mitigate confounds." Meta-analysis of test-retest effects shows practice effects as common source of bias in repeated memory testing.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** With 4 tests across 6 days, participants complete progressively more VR trials. If congruent items are easier (require less practice to master), older adults might show faster practice asymptote for congruent items, creating false appearance of schema-mediated preservation. Incongruent items (harder) might show slower practice asymptote in older adults specifically, confounding age effects.
- **Strength:** CRITICAL (if not adequately addressed in analysis plan)
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "The analysis addresses practice effects through IRT theta scoring, which separates item difficulty parameters from ability estimates across sessions. By estimating a single latent ability trajectory for each participant across all sessions, theta scores remove session-specific difficulty inflation from practice. However, if age groups differ in learning curve shapes (e.g., older adults plateau faster), this could confound Age × Time interactions. Recommendation: After fitting primary model, examine residual plots by age group and test session to verify practice effects are adequately removed. If heterogeneous learning curves detected, include test session × age interaction as covariate or use robust regression."

**[2] VR Simulator Sickness Differential Dropout and Selection Bias**

- **Confound Description:** VR sickness causes approximately 15.6% mean dropout in published studies (meta-analysis), with higher rates in older adults specifically. Non-random dropout would bias the final age distribution, creating selection bias in the sample composition.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If older adults drop out due to sickness at higher rates than younger adults, the "older adult" sample surviving to Day 6 may be systematically different (more sickness-resistant, possibly with better overall memory function). This would bias age effect estimates. Additionally, if certain task types (navigation-heavy) induce more sickness in older adults, domain-specific dropout could bias domain × age effects.
- **Literature Evidence:** Meta-analysis of VR sickness (2020-2024): "mean dropout rate across 46 experiments was 15.6%" and "older group (70-90 years) showed higher dropout than younger group (21-50 years, Park et al. 2006)." Study on cybersickness: "65.2% of participants experienced cybersickness, 23.9% severely." Longitudinal study (German VRSQ): "all participants completed the entire simulation" (suggesting careful design can minimize dropout).
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** The REMEMVR design involves 4 sessions across 6 days—multiple opportunities for dropout due to sickness. If older adults withdraw more frequently, the final sample's age stratification would be compromised, and measured "age effects" could reflect survivor bias rather than true age differences.
- **Strength:** MODERATE (depends on actual dropout rate in REMEMVR; methods.md reports "No participants reported nausea, disorientation, or discomfort")
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "The thesis methods (Chapter 2) report that no participants reported adverse VR effects, and study design minimized sickness risk through 1:1 real-world movement mapping and supervised sessions. However, systematic tracking of simulator sickness symptoms (via SSQ questionnaire) during formal data collection is recommended. If dropout did occur, compare completer vs. non-completer characteristics by age group to test for selection bias. Results should report final sample composition vs. intended 10/decade stratification."
