#### Commission Errors (Critiques of Claims Made)

**1. Oversimplification of Recognition Advantage**
- **Location:** Section 2: Theoretical Background - Familiarity vs Recollection paragraph
- **Claim Made:** "Recognition can succeed via familiarity (fast, automatic), while Free Recall requires effortful recollection. This predicts Recognition will be most resistant to forgetting."
- **Scholarly Criticism:** This oversimplifies the dual-process dynamics. Research shows familiarity actually has faster forgetting at SHORT intervals (seconds to minutes), but becomes more stable than recollection over LONGER intervals (days to weeks). The prediction may be correct but the mechanism described is incomplete.
- **Counterevidence:** Yonelinas (2002, J Exp Psychol Gen) and replicated in Xu et al. (2016, Frontiers in Psychology) found that familiarity shows rapid decay over short retention intervals but stabilizes, while recollection is the main source of long-term forgetting.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** "Clarify that the prediction applies to the 6-day timescale of the study, where recollection decay dominates. At this timescale, recognition's familiarity component should provide advantage precisely because familiarity is stable after initial encoding period."

**2. Implied Linearity of Retrieval Support Continuum**
- **Location:** Section 3: Hypothesis - Expected Effect Pattern
- **Claim Made:** "Effect sizes at Day 6: Free vs Cued d ~ 0.3-0.4, Free vs Recognition d ~ 0.6-0.8"
- **Scholarly Criticism:** These specific effect size predictions imply roughly linear spacing on the retrieval support continuum (Cued is approximately halfway between Free and Recognition). However, the continuum may not be linear - Cued Recall may be much closer to Free Recall in demands than to Recognition.
- **Counterevidence:** Memory & Cognition (2023) variability study found Cued Recall performance is more variable than Free Recall, suggesting Cued Recall occupies an unstable position on the continuum, not a smooth midpoint.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Rebuttal:** "Effect size predictions are exploratory estimates. The hypothesis tests monotonic ordering, not specific spacing. Non-linear relationships are anticipated and exploratory analyses can characterize the actual gradient shape."

#### Omission Errors (Missing Context or Claims)

**1. No Discussion of Practice Effects Across Paradigms**
- **Missing Content:** The concept document does not acknowledge that repeated testing with the same paradigms may create paradigm-specific practice effects
- **Why It Matters:** Free Recall may benefit more from practice (participants learn optimal retrieval strategies) than Recognition (already at ceiling). This could compress apparent forgetting differences over sessions.
- **Supporting Literature:** VR memory assessment literature (Nature Scientific Reports, 2024) explicitly identifies practice effects as a concern in longitudinal VR testing, with effects varying by task type.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you distinguish paradigm-specific forgetting from paradigm-specific practice effects? Did Free Recall improve more across sessions than Recognition?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 5: Analysis Approach - acknowledge potential practice effects, note that IRT theta scoring partially controls for item-level learning (separates ability from difficulty), and that all paradigms are tested at same timepoints (controlling for session effects)."

**2. Ceiling Effects in Recognition Not Addressed**
- **Missing Content:** Recognition typically shows higher baseline accuracy than Recall paradigms. This may create ceiling effects limiting observable forgetting.
- **Why It Matters:** If Recognition starts at ceiling (e.g., 95% accuracy at Day 0), apparent "slow forgetting" may reflect measurement artifacts rather than true memory advantage.
- **Supporting Literature:** Signal Detection-IRT models (PMC, 2018) explicitly note that ceiling effects in recognition memory "have not been fully explored" but represent a significant measurement concern. IRT addresses this but limitations should be acknowledged.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "Is Recognition's flat trajectory due to memory resilience or ceiling effects masking decay?"
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Addition:** "Add to Section 5: Analysis Approach - note that IRT theta scoring mitigates ceiling effects by estimating latent ability rather than raw accuracy. Additionally, the 2-pass purification removes items with extreme difficulty parameters, including ceiling-prone items."

**3. No Mention of Encoding Specificity Alternative**
- **Missing Content:** The transfer-appropriate processing framework emphasizes encoding-retrieval match, but the encoding specificity principle (Tulving & Thomson, 1973) offers an alternative perspective that effective retrieval cues depend on what was encoded.
- **Why It Matters:** Under encoding specificity, paradigm differences might reflect differences in how well retrieval cues match encoded traces, not retrieval support per se.
- **Supporting Literature:** Dynamic Cueing research (PMC, 2025) challenges classic encoding specificity by showing effective cues must engage the current state of memory, not just original encoding.
- **Potential Reviewer Question:** "How do you distinguish retrieval support effects from encoding-specificity effects? All paradigms use the same encoding, but does that guarantee equal encoding specificity?"
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Addition:** "This is adequately addressed by the design: identical incidental encoding eliminates encoding differences. The retrieval support hypothesis specifically tests whether retrieval demands (not encoding) drive trajectory differences."

#### Alternative Theoretical Frameworks (Not Considered)

**1. Single-Process Signal Detection Model**
- **Alternative Theory:** Wixted & Squire (2010, 2011) argue that recollection and familiarity reflect the same underlying form of memory at different strength levels, not qualitatively distinct processes.
- **How It Applies:** Under single-process theory, Recognition vs. Recall differences might reflect strength gradients rather than qualitative dual-process distinctions. The "familiarity advantage" might simply be stronger initial encoding for items tested via recognition.
- **Key Citation:** Dunn (2004, Psychological Review) and Wixted & Squire (2010, Psychological Review) - single-process challenges to dual-process theory
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Reviewers familiar with the signal detection vs. dual-process debate will expect acknowledgment of this alternative.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "Add brief note in Theoretical Background: 'While dual-process theory guides our predictions, single-process alternatives (Wixted & Squire, 2010) attribute paradigm differences to memory strength rather than distinct processes. Our design cannot definitively distinguish these accounts but tests whether paradigm-specific trajectories exist regardless of underlying mechanism.'"

**2. Encoding Quality Differences Across Paradigms**
- **Alternative Theory:** Items that will be tested via recognition may receive different encoding emphasis than items tested via recall, even with "identical" encoding.
- **How It Applies:** If participants (unconsciously or otherwise) encode visual details more deeply for recognition-tested items, observed trajectory differences might reflect encoding quality, not retrieval support.
- **Key Citation:** Bonnici et al. (2022, Hippocampus) showed spatial context encoded with greater hippocampal engagement than temporal context in VR.
- **Why Concept.md Should Address It:** Challenges the assumption that incidental encoding guarantees equal encoding across paradigms.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Acknowledgment:** "The incidental encoding design specifically addresses this concern - participants do not know which paradigm will test which items during encoding, eliminating strategic encoding differences."

#### Known Methodological Confounds (Unaddressed)

**1. Differential Ceiling/Floor Effects Across Paradigms**
- **Confound Description:** Recognition typically has higher accuracy (potential ceiling) while Free Recall has lower accuracy (potential floor). These asymmetric distributions may create artifactual trajectory differences.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** If Recognition starts near ceiling and can't go higher, while Free Recall starts lower with more room to decline, trajectories may appear to "converge" rather than "diverge" as predicted.
- **Literature Evidence:** IRT-based measurement (Gibbons et al., 2024, Alzheimer's & Dementia) explicitly addresses ceiling/floor effects in cognitive testing through latent trait estimation.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** The predicted "fan-out" pattern depends on Recognition maintaining high performance while Free Recall declines - but if Recognition is at ceiling, the pattern could be masked.
- **Strength:** MODERATE
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "The concept document's use of IRT theta scoring directly addresses this: IRT estimates latent ability across the full continuum, and the 2-pass purification removes items with extreme parameters. Report theta score distributions at each timepoint to verify no ceiling/floor artifacts."

**2. Response Format Differences Between Paradigms**
- **Confound Description:** Free Recall uses free-text responses, Cued Recall uses partial cues, and Recognition uses multiple-choice. Different response formats may introduce format-specific biases.
- **How It Could Affect Results:** Free-text scoring may be more variable (spelling, partial credit) than multiple-choice, introducing noise that could affect trajectory estimation.
- **Literature Evidence:** The methods.md document explicitly describes different scoring procedures for free-text vs. multi-choice responses, including partial credit rules.
- **Why Relevant to This RQ:** Scoring differences could create paradigm-specific measurement error, affecting trajectory comparison.
- **Strength:** MINOR
- **Suggested Mitigation:** "This is adequately addressed by restricting to Item-level paradigms (IFR, ICR, IRE) which share common scoring procedures for the item identity questions. The dichotomization for IRT further standardizes scoring across paradigms."
